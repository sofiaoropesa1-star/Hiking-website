Introduction to Hiking

Hiking means walking through forests, mountains, deserts, and parks. Anyone can enjoy it without special skills.

It strengthens your body, reduces stress, and lets you experience nature up close. Make sure to start with easy trails and gradually increase difficulty.

Mountain Hiking

Mountain hiking offers breathtaking views and challenging climbs.

  • High altitude can affect breathing.
  • Steep climbs require strong leg muscles.
  • Cooler air at the top, so dress in layers.
  • Check weather carefully; conditions can change quickly.

Canyon Trails

Canyon hiking offers deep pathways carved by nature over millions of years.

  • Steep rocky walls can be slippery.
  • Shaded paths keep you cool, but be careful with loose rocks.
  • Famous in the Southwest U.S., like the Grand Canyon.

Forest Trails

Forests provide peaceful scenery filled with wildlife and plant life.

  • Lush green surroundings.
  • Soft dirt pathways are easier on the feet.
  • Great shade on sunny days.
  • Watch for roots and uneven terrain.

How to Prepare

Research your trail, check weather, dress in layers, and pack essentials like water and snacks.

  • Bring a map or GPS device.
  • Wear proper hiking shoes.
  • Tell someone your plan and expected return time.

Dangers of Hiking

Weather can change quickly, wildlife may appear, and dehydration can happen. Stay alert and follow safety rules.

  • Watch for sudden storms.
  • Be aware of snakes, insects, and wildlife.
  • Carry first aid and know how to use it.

What to Do If You Get Lost

Stay calm and use the STOP method: Stop, Think, Observe, Plan. Make yourself visible and signal for help.

  • Stay where you are if possible.
  • Use a whistle or reflective gear to signal.
  • Conserve energy and water.
